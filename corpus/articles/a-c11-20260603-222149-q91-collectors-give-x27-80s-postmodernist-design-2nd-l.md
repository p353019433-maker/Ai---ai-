"Super Lamp" by Martine BedinVictoria and Albert Museum In the film version of "American Psycho," the controversial novel by Bret Easton Ellis, the camera floats down the hallway of an Upper West Side apartment that is meticulously - and minimally - appointed with some of the best examples of 1980s design. In one corner, a Mackintosh Hill House chair, while in the living room, a "Men in the Cities" print by Robert Longo. Together with a pair of Mies Van der Rohe Barcelona chairs, the apartment could just as easily be a set piece for an early Calvin Klein ad. But it's not. It's the apartment of an investment banker turned sociopathic killer, in 1980s New York, when lavish living was expressed with postmodernist art and design. At its peak, it included San Francisco's 580 California St. building designed by Philip Johnson (1984), famously topped with those haunting statues of "Corporate Goddesses." Perhaps most misunderstood and openly loathed, however, was Memphis, a school of design that was a riot of color and materials that often overwhelmed a piece's original intent, a shotgun wedding between Bauhaus and Fisher-Price. SF Gate Logo But PoMo (as it was sometimes unaffectionately nicknamed) is getting a second look these days, and that's being helped in large part by a major exhibition at London's Victoria and Albert Museum, "Postmodernism: Style and Subversion, 1970-1990," which ended today. The show has already ruffled feathers from critics who argue that postmodernism was a joke, an aberration of modernism that left us with a glut of curlicued sea foam green and teal office buildings and, of course, a plethora of Patrick Nagel prints. Advertisement Article continues below this ad "Postmodernism is still a volatile subject for many people," says Jane Pavitt, co-curator of the V&A exhibition. "In its most exuberant phase, it was a provocative call for diversity and plurality in the arts, and in society as a whole." Diving off the anarchy of punk rock and early new wave, artists and designers made postmodernist design an expression of the era's flamboyance and decadence, with a generous shot of rebellion: Vivienne Westwood's ironic take on S&M and bondage, Ron Arad's Concrete Stereo (1983), and the Memphis group's wildly bizarre furniture and accessories. "Postmodernism, quite simply, was image-friendly and always cover-ready," Pavitt says. "And it's an enduring example of how design practice can be both provocative and challenging." Advertisement Article continues below this ad Living in the '80s When it trickled down to the masses, it gained broader acceptance with its graphic drama and angular geometry. Ken McGavin and Katie Cobler run the 80s Gallery, an online store that has seen a spike in sales of all things '80s. "Whether it's a neon Empire State Building lamp or an original Patrick Nagel painting, people are contacting us for very specific items," McGavin says. Indeed, Nagel - the epitome of bachelor pad chic and Duran Duran album covers - is making something of a comeback. McGavin and Cobler are hardly hobbyists when it comes to '80s style. Their home in Paso Robles is an unflinching homage to that decade of indulgence. "The 1980s was a period that didn't apologize for bold design," Cobler says. "When people look back at the '80s, they tend to remember themselves wearing shoulder pads and side ponytails. They can't separate their own questionable fashion choices from a revolutionary movement of art and design." Advertisement Article continues below this ad After a decade or more of hubbub over modernism, not to mention skyrocketing prices for Art Deco, there are now plenty of collectors giving PoMo and '80s pop art more than a passing glance. "Postmodern design is inclusive - it appeals to both the design cognoscenti as well as the popular culture in that it's fun and whimsical," says Jason Stein, associate director of 20th century arts at Bonham's. In just the past year, Bonham's has seen a remarkable return on postmodern pieces. A teapot by Peter Shire (1984) sold for $3,750, while a John Lewis cast glass sculptural table (1985) sold for $10,980. A pair of candlesticks by Ettore Sottsass, considered the father of Memphis design, sold for just over a thousand dollars. New market Compared with the prices fetched for midcentury pieces, PoMo looks like a bargain - but that probably will not be for long. Advertisement Article continues below this ad "Holy grail pieces of this period include Shiro Kuramata's Miss Blanche resin and aluminum chair and Marc Newson's aluminum Lockheed Lounge," Stein says. The latter piece has been the accessory of choice for many high-profile photo shoots, and most recently was featured in the September issue of Vanity Fair, with Angelina Jolie languidly reclining on the aluminum beauty. In 2010, a 1988 Lockheed Lounge prototype sold for $2.1 million. Nevertheless, there's still plenty of style to go around. "Over the past few years, we've been working to source and collect original Nagel paintings, especially his larger-format original paintings on canvas," Cobler says.
