Bryan Robertson | | The Guardian Skip to main contentSkip to navigation This article is more than 23 years old Obituary Bryan Robertson This article is more than 23 years old Distinguished director who turned London's Whitechapel art gallery into a beacon of new work Bryan Robertson, who has died aged 77, was an art director in the major league, like Alfred Barr of the Museum of Modern Art, and James Johnson Sweeney of the Guggenheim, both in New York, Pontus Hulten at the Beaubourg in Paris, and Sir Nicholas Serota at the Tate in London. Yet Robertson never headed a major international gallery. He had been widely tipped to take over at the old Tate gallery when Sir John Rothenstein retired in 1964. However, after the agitation of heavy politicking behind the scenes by the ambitious painter, art historian and educationist Lawrence Gowing on his own behalf, the trustees selected Norman Reid, thought of as the ultimate safe pair of hands - and rather more, as it turned out - thus delivering a blow for which Robertson was never quite able to forgive either Gowing or life. It can never be known whether Robertson would have had the vision and drive, like Serota, to have created and pushed through a project as huge as Tate Modern - in any case, the arts were not awash with lottery money in the 1960s - but the exhibitions he curated at the Whitechapel art gallery, in the unlikely setting of London's East End, during a turbulent period in world art, were, if anything, even more influential. They included the first British one-man shows for Jackson Pollock (1958) and Mark Rothko (1961), and the exhibition This Is Tomorrow (1956), featuring Brits like Eduardo Paolozzi and Richard Hamilton, whose collage Just What Is It That Makes Today's Homes So Different, So Appealing? was seized upon as the first manifestation of the pop art later taken over lock, stock and Lichtenstein by the Americans. Robertson's shows captured the imagination of a generation of artists, established and aspiring, and future gallery directors, including Serota, who, after eight years of financial consolidation at the Whitechapel, in 1976 followed Robertson into the directorship - and, like Robertson, made his name there. He had begun going to the Whitechapel at the age of 18 in 1964, and still remembers the galvanising effect on him of Robertson's exhibitions of Robert Rauschenberg and, the following year, of Franz Kline, both deeply influential American artists of the time, whose reputations outlived their century. Robertson was born in London to an unmarried couple (no shame to him; he included that nugget in his Who's Who entry). He was brought up in the capital, and, but for short spells in Germany, France and the United States, lived there throughout his life. His formal education went no further than Battersea grammar school, but, as schools go, it went a long way, and he never ceased to be grateful for the grounding it gave him, not least in pagan and Christian mythology, from Ovid to the Bible, which later enabled him to understand the complexity of Renaissance paintings, whose subjects are lost on later generations benefiting from advanced education practices that dispensed with the classics. Even Picasso, after all, often drew from the well of Ovid's Metarmorphoses. Education remained at the centre of Robertson's life project. His grandfather had been the headmaster of a school in Holborn for slum children, and the grandson never neglected the importance of art to children. As he reminded Guardian readers in an article in 1995, he worked through the 50s and 60s for the Society for Education Through Art, and, during his 16 years at the Whitechapel, he held annual shows of art for schoolchildren. But he first found his feet after studying and working on the continent for a couple of years after the second world war, when, in 1949, he became director of the Heffer gallery in Cambridge. Three years later, at the age of 27, he won the Whitechapel job. If the longterm prospect was thrilling, the immediate impact of the gallery on its new director was dismal. It looked, he was to say later, like a rundown soup kitchen, and when he tried to coax an annual grant out of the Arts Council, he was told that, as he was on the wrong side of Aldgate from the City of London, he was technically a provincial gallery and entitled only to occasional grants. The Whitechapel had been founded in Jack the Ripper's time - and on his manor - to get people out of the pubs and off the street, and show them some culture in a warm, brightly lit, welcoming environment. The enlightened founders procured, from the architect Harrison Townsend, a splendid arts and crafts style building; but more importantly from the public point of view, to this day is the fact that visitors walk straight off the street into the gallery on the same level. There are no flights of steps, no portico - it is the equivalent to Anthony Caro removing sculpture from its plinth, and placing it on the floor; and Caro was one of the up-and-coming British artists to have a one-man show at Robertson's Whitechapel. Through the first half of the century, the gallery had made a reputation for international class shows, culminating in 1939 with the exhibition of Picasso's Guernica, on its way to Moma, the Museum of Modern Art in New York, for security after Franco's triumph in the Spanish civil war; it has never again been shown in Britain. But when Robertson arrived, the second world war had scattered the old Whitechapel public, and the gallery had fallen on hard times. What it now needed was an entrepreneurial spirit. With Robertson, it got it. He did all the right things, from ripping the hessian off the walls and painting the galleries brilliant white, to mounting a show of JMW Turner that was a sensational reintroduction to an artist who had been heavily put down in the 1920s by the Bloomsbury critic and Cézanne-fancier Roger Fry. He followed up with exhibitions that began to make Rothenstein's Tate look like the fading soup kitchen, and the press took pleasure in saying so. "I rather liked the old boy," Robertson was to write, "and I knew that his annual fund for purchases amounted to a paltry £800." He might have rather liked the old boy, but he energetically pressed home his superiority as a curator with shows not just of the big Americans, but of Prunella Clough, Keith Vaughan, Edward Burra and Ceri Richards. In the 1950s, Barbara Hepworth's career was in decline, partly because she was in St Ives before St Ives became the glass of fashion, partly because she was a woman - the British at this time were suspicious of women artists in general, and downright disapproving of women sculptors in particular. But the retrospective Robertson gave her found Hepworth a new and enlightened West End gallery, Gimpel Fils, and revitalised her career. He was, in the words of Penelope Curtis, author of a study of the sculptor, "one of Hepworth's most effective postwar champions". In 1954, Robertson saw the great Diaghilev exhibition in London mounted as a totality, an environment, by the dance critic Richard Buckle. Two years later, he masterminded This Is Tomorrow, a show put together in 12 parts but cohering as an environment, and featuring a group that had met frequently at the old Institute of Contemporary Arts in Dover Street to discuss a modern urban art drawing on advertising, comic strips, movies, science fiction - an art that was to be named Pop Art by the critic Lawrence Alloway. Now, Robertson's exhibition included the work and ideas of, apart from Hamilton and Paolozzi, Peter and Alison Smithson, the brutalist architects, and a scattering of other architects, teachers and critics. Robertson always wrote well about art, and it is plain to see that he shared with another critic/curator of his generation, David Sylvester, those essential qualities for a maker of exhibitions: a good eye, an eclectic taste, and an enthusiasm for the new.
