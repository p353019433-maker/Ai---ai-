New St Paul’s Cathedral identity blends past and present Skip to content St Paul’s Cathedral is arguably London’s most iconic landmark, having dominated the city’s skyline since its completion over 300 years ago. A vital religious site and a symbol of resilience during the Blitz, it has long played an important role in defining Britain’s sense of identity. Now, this famous old building has been given a visual refresh, reflecting both its rich history and its evolving role as a cultural institution. Designed by Sir Christopher Wren in the late 17th century, St Paul’s today operates as both an active place of worship and one of the capital’s most visited attractions. Its new branding, created by Pentagram, aims to reconcile these dual roles while looking to the future. Commissioned following the Cathedral’s renewed focus on accessibility and public engagement, the project extends beyond visual design into strategy and language. Pentagram collaborated with strategists and writers to develop a tone of voice centred on openness, anchored by the idea that St Paul’s is “for everyone”: a space for reflection, learning and gathering. All images © St Paul’s Cathedral / Pentagram The visual identity itself draws inspiration from the building’s fabric. A newly developed wordmark borrows typographic details found within the Cathedral, from engravings in the stonework to references uncovered in the Crypt. Pentagram worked closely with stonemasons to explore how these historic forms could inform a contemporary mark without losing their authenticity, in doing so bridging centuries of visual language. Elevated letterforms within the wordmark nod to archival references discovered in the Cathedral’s library, while a monogram distilled from its characters provides a flexible shorthand across applications. Typography further supports this balance of old and new: Dinamo’s Arizona Flare, with its classic letterforms, is complemented by a secondary typeface, Raleway, for clarity across body text and subheadings. Meanwhile, colour and imagery extend the identity into a more immersive system. A palette inspired by the Cathedral’s interior architecture – from muted stone hues to vibrant ceiling mosaics and wall hangings – reinforces a sense of place, while photography captures both its monumental scale and quieter, more intimate moments. For Pentagram partner Domenic Lippa, the challenge lay in capturing something less obviously tangible. “The ambition for us at Pentagram, the strategists and writers, as well as the client themselves, was to create an identity that would be authentic, contemporary, flexible and dynamic,” he says. “To be true for a unique building that exists so proudly in London, but also reflects many of the strengths of our country.” He adds: “I genuinely believe the building has a special personality, and we needed to try to capture this.” The resulting system is intentionally adaptable, designed to evolve alongside the Cathedral’s activities and audiences. “We hope we have created a fluid and flexible identity that will feel appropriate to the cathedral,” Lippa continues, “but like the building itself, we hope it will surprise and delight.” In doing so, the rebrand positions St Paul’s not as an enduring yet lifeless monument, but as a living community hub that continues both to shape, and be
