Rossetto, whose Wired of the 1990s was both breathtaking and groundbreaking in its use of neon colors and outrageous graphics, helped bring the magazine's once-flat look in line with its provocative content. Rossetto is no longer a working stiff in the magazine business, but he has been reading Reason since shortly after the magazine launched in 1968 as a mimeographed sheet run by a Boston University student, and he wanted to help the magazine reach more readers. "I've looked at business plans and editorial prototypes and offered comments on the rare occasions I've been asked, but not like with Reason. Reason is special," Rossetto wrote yesterday in an e-mail from Amsterdam. "It's one of the reasons I got involved in magazines to begin with, way back when it started. "It was inspiring to me because it spoke bright, sometimes uncomfortable truths, and it looked really smart. And since I personally knew the guy who was doing it, I realized for the first time that making magazines wasn't something that could only be done by an isolated elite, that commitment and talent made a difference." Remember, Rossetto and his wife, Jane Metcalfe, were living in Amsterdam and publishing a magazine called Electronic Word when they had the idea for Wired in the early 1990s. Although many doors slammed in their faces, they persevered and built what appears to be a lasting brand. "Clearly, Wired was the magazine of the '90s," said Reason editor Nick Gillespie. "It completely changed the way people thought about magazines." Gillespie had been a Reason staffer since 1993, when he was working on a doctorate in American Literature. He has the Ph.D., became editor in January 2000 and began thinking about how to update the look. By December, Rossetto was thinking the same thing. He liked some of the changes Gillespie had instituted -- more cultural stories, longer book reviews, and humor pieces -- and thought the magazine could use a new look. Rossetto wrote to Bob Poole, the founder of the Reason Foundation -- the nonprofit organization that owns Reason magazine -- and suggested a redesign. Gillespie was thrilled. He met with Rossetto and Metcalfe at their Berkeley offices last December, and Rossetto put Gillespie, Reason Publisher Mike Alissi and art director Barb Burch in touch with accomplished designers Erik Spiekermann and Susanna Dulkinys. Spiekermann, the inventor of the typeface Meta, which Rossetto says has replaced Helvetica as "the most popular sans serif face in the world," was just coming off the redesign of the Economist. Dulkinys had worked for Rossetto at Wired. From then on, Rossetto said, his own involvement was nominal. The new look is cleaner, more modern, making use of the Meta typeface throughout. In at least one important way, the "new" Reason is a perfect heir to the Wired legacy: Its offices are virtual. Gillespie is based in Oxford, Ohio, where his wife works as a college professor; Alissi, the publisher, is in Connecticut; Burch is in Phoenix; and others on the staff of only 13 people are scattered from Los Angeles to Washington, D.C. But Reason is also an ideological cousin to Wired, in a way. "Wired wasn't a technology magazine. It never covered feeds and speeds, " Rossetto said. "It covered the digital revolution. That revolution isn't over. Hardly, it's just begun, and the changes to come will dwarf everything that's come so far." Rossetto said the digital revolution makes its impact felt on everything, including politics, economics and culture. "And by politics, I don't just mean electoral politics, I mean democracy. Billion dollar campaigns are not democracy. Rather, democracy as the Greeks defined it was about how people lived with each other, in their families, communities, states," he said. "That's why when Jane and I started Wired, we had an editor who exclusively covered the political implications of the digital revolution. As soon as Jane and I left, he was fired. Should Wired cover democracy now? You'll have to ask the people who are in charge now. What I do know is that at least someone is, and I'm glad it's Reason." . SALON STRUGGLES: Salon Media Group reported yet another dismal quarter yesterday as the San Francisco online firm clings to life in a horrible advertising environment. Salon lost $2.4 million (42 cents per share) on revenue of $567,000 for the second quarter that ended Sept. 30, a drop of 74 percent from the year earlier. Chief executive Michael O'Donnell said in a statement that he was so busy raising money that ad sales suffered. In addition, he said, "Customer orders dropped off substantially with the worsening economy and growing uncertainty from customers about Salon's future." As bleak as that sounds, Salon found some bright spots: Some advertisers, such as IBM, HBO, Absolut and Universal Studios, came back. O'Donnell did raise about $3.2 million, giving the company a cash cushion. It continued to trim expenses, spending about $2.9 million in the quarter, roughly half what it spent a year earlier. People continued to read the site, to the tune of 3.8 million unique users, up 46 percent from a year earlier. Salon is one of the sites trying to get cash directly out of those users. So far, 25,000 pay for either Salon Premium or the Well. Subscribers accounted for $254,000 in the quarter, almost half of all revenue. It's a start.
