Show MoreShow Less 3of3 At first, Carol Sklenicka didn't set out to write a biography of the late American short story master, Raymond Carver, but as in so many of Carver's stories, things just seemed to happen as if they were meant to be. In the mid-'90s, the Santa Maria native was writing fiction and teaching at Marquette University in Milwaukee when she traveled to Tacoma, Wash., to give a reading. "I had nothing to do for the rest of the weekend," she says in a recent interview at KQED after an appearance on "Forum." "I had this rental car and it was pouring rain and I did this thing that my dad used to do, which was to just drive around and go stay in a motel somewhere. I ended up in Yakima, and Yakima kind of spoke to me." Two weeks later, back in Wisconsin, she came across photographer Bob Adelman's book "Carver Country" and thought, " 'Oh, Raymond Carver is from Yakima.' " Still not quite planning what would become an exhaustively researched and definitive biography, Sklenicka decided to interview some of the people who knew Carver. "I started coming out to the Northwest about twice a year, rounding up whoever would talk to me," she says. "At some point it occurred to me that I should write a biography of his life." After about 10 years, that recently published biography, "Raymond Carver: A Writer's Life" (578 pages, Scribner), has earned wide critical praise and was just named one of the best nonfiction books of the year by both The Chronicle and the New York Times. Quiet determination An easygoing woman with short graying hair and a ready smile, Sklenicka exudes the kind of quiet determination that sustained her through interviews with about 300 sources as she traced the meandering course of Carver's life from his childhood in Washington, his years in Northern California, and his return to Washington where he finally had a decade of sobriety before dying of lung cancer at 50 in 1988. Along the way, Carver became one of the most influential writers of the late 20th century, with collections such as "Will You Please Be Quiet, Please?," "Furious Seasons" and "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love." The stories - marked by a unique voice and a kind of poetic brutality - are populated by characters drawn as if they were standing beneath naked light bulbs. Many stories focused on relationships between men and women and drew on his own alcohol-fueled but deeply passionate first marriage to his high school sweetheart, Maryann, who called them his "he/she stories." Talking to KQED'S Michael Krasny, Sklenicka, 61, quoted one source as advising her that if she wanted to know about Carver's life, she need only read his stories. Yet while it's true he unsparingly mined his own life and experiences for his work, the stories were still works of perfectly crafted fiction. As an example, Sklenicka cites the story "Tell the Women We're Going," in which two men go out for a ride and end up raping and murdering two women. "Carver said that story came from riding around with some older guys when he was a kid," Sklenicka says. "The guys said, 'Hey here comes some women, let's go rape 'em.' He was shocked, of course. It didn't happen, but the story happened." For Carver, the frequent bridges between his life and his fiction were the "what-ifs" - taking a real experience and asking himself what would have happened if he or someone he knew had taken a different path, made a different decision. One story tells of a man traveling in Europe to meet his son, as Carver did when his son, Vance, was living in Germany. In the story, the man decides not to get off the train to meet his son, but in real life, Vance had a brief reunion with his father. Not only does the story show how Carver considered "what-ifs," it also reveals the complicated mix of love and resentment Carver felt toward his son and daughter. "He used the raw material of his life in terms of people," Sklenicka says. "In fact, he often called his characters his people - he didn't make that distinction between characters and people the way authors often do." Beyond category Although his work was sometimes wrongly linked to the late-century literary trend called minimalism, Carver never thought of himself as a minimalist or in any category, for that matter. "He rejected categories generally," Sklenicka says. "I don't think he had an abstract mind at all. He just wasn't built that way, which is why he's so good at picking the right details that will stand for many things." If many of the stories seem to embody some of the tenets of minimalism, it's largely because of how they were edited by Gordon Lish, once the fiction editor at Esquire and a controlling figure in Carver's career. Notably, he lopped off endings in Carver's stories, giving them a certain "shaggy dog" structure. Some see Lish as the bogeyman in Carver's life who was able to reshape the fiction largely because Carver's alcoholism made him overly dependent and afraid to stand up for himself. "The Lish thing is complicated," Sklenicka says. "I think Lish does great line editing, but I don't like what he does to the arc of the stories - the early ones would have benefited from that line editing. "It's important to look at the fuller, the more generous stories - that's what (second wife) Tess (Gallagher) calls them," she continues. "The more generous endings (which show up in the later stories) were there from the beginning, but they did not come about just because he was sober and had a new life. They came about because he was allowed to keep the endings he was writing." Even after Carver and Maryann divorced and he married poet Gallagher, Carver continued to use his first marriage as a source for new stories - "Maryann herself has a sense that their relationship never ended," Sklenicka says. "Ray talked about it as an obsession. It was an obsession." Maryann Carver, Lish, Gallagher and others were part of a "nest of people Carver had that kept him from completely falling apart and that made it possible for him to ... hold himself together enough to use his imagination. ... All his friends kind of took care of him. They loved him. They seemed to put up with him enormously." Some got away Sklenicka was able to secure interviews with many key players in Carver's life and career, but a few were unavailable. Author Richard Ford, a former student of Carver's who became his close friend, declined to be interviewed, but did allow Sklenicka to use his correspondence with Carver.
