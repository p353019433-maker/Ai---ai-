## 1. Interior architecture: building from the inside out

Interior architecture is not interior decoration. The useful definition is “designing a building from the inside out”: the interior is where structure, use, human occupancy, atmosphere, and adaptive reuse meet. It can refer to original interior planning, later redesign, or the adaptive reuse of an existing shell.

The design lesson is that space is not only envelope. The interior is a behavioral system: circulation, light, services, furniture, openings, surfaces, acoustics, storage, and ritual. Adaptive reuse also gives it an ecological dimension: conserving resources by reworking existing structures instead of replacing them.

Rule: when designing any product/UI environment, treat the inside as the primary site of use. External form should answer internal occupation, not merely image.

Source: priority 273, `https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_architecture`.

## 2. British modernity: Wells Coates, Concorde, Brunel

Wells Coates shows modernism as social and technical reorganization. His Lawn Road flats, electrical products, broadcasting studios, cinemas, heaters, televisions, and yachts all come from a belief that architecture should answer new social/economic conditions. His quote is the core: a new architecture was necessary because society was in transition. Modernism here is not “white box style”; it is a response to new ways of living, media, mobility, and technical production.

Concorde extends design into international engineering collaboration. It was an Anglo-French system: aircraft body, engine development, testing, maintenance, responsibility distribution, national prestige, and passenger experience all linked. The object is iconic, but its design meaning is collaborative complexity and engineering coordination.

Brunel adds the 19th-century infrastructure ancestor: railways, bridges, docks, ships, and prefabricated hospitals. His work matters as systems engineering before modern design language. He shows that scale, boldness, and execution are design virtues when they reshape movement, trade, and public life.

Rule: infrastructure design is not just object design scaled up; it is coordination across politics, engineering, use, maintenance, and cultural imagination.

Sources: priority 355 Wells Coates; 359 Concorde; 362 Isambard Kingdom Brunel.

## 3. Game and interactive worlds: Rockstar Games

The Rockstar Games source is useful because it frames `Grand Theft Auto: Vice City` as design beyond graphics. The important claim is that the game overcame traditional video-game limits: linear narrative, condensed space, and pre-determined activities. It created a seductive world of characters, props, locations, narratives, and player choice.

The lesson for interaction design is that world-building is an interface problem. Good game design coordinates freedom and constraint: the player feels agency because the world is dense enough to respond, but structured enough to remain readable.

Rule: in interactive systems, design the choice architecture, not just the surface. Props, environments, missions, characters, feedback, and pacing are all UI.

Source: priority 361, Rockstar Games / Design Museum.

## 4. Social and humanitarian design: Cameron Sinclair, &made, Nadine Jarvis

Cameron Sinclair / Architecture for Humanity frames design as a response to social and humanitarian crises. The important pattern is not charity aesthetics; it is a global network of designers/architects proposing and executing design solutions for refugees, disaster reconstruction, schools, clinics, and community infrastructure. The phrase “design like you give a damn” matters because it moves design ethics from sentiment to operational model.

&made turns social/environmental issues into object proposals. Their `Lost & Found` work uses salvaged furniture and reclaimed timber to discuss waste/excess. Their `Climatised Objects` respond to climate disaster by embedding dual functionality into desirable domestic objects that can become life-saving devices. This is a sharp design move: the emergency function is latent in everyday form.

Nadine Jarvis works on immateriality, impermanence, death, and emotional consequence. Receptacles for crematory ashes, bird feeders, and decomposing materials turn absence into object behavior. This shows that product design can handle grief, temporality, and ritual without becoming sentimental decoration.

Rule: social design is strongest when ethics becomes structure, affordance, network, or lifecycle — not a caption explaining good intentions.

Sources: priority 363 Cameron Sinclair; 502 &made; 603 Nadine Jarvis.

## 5. New craft, material intelligence, and computational fabrication

Tord Boontje combines advanced technologies with artisanal techniques to create glassware, lighting, furniture, and romantic/ornamental objects. His work is useful because it rejects the false split between craft and technology: computer-generated floral drawings, Swarovski chandeliers, recycled bottle work, and mass-market Habitat lights sit on a continuum.

Viable represents a return from amorphous forms toward hard edges and sophisticated geometries. The source explicitly ties geometry to a cultural desire for stability and to the challenge of making precise objects whose formation may be computationally complex. Geometry here is not retro minimalism; it is a way of making complexity legible.

Sam Buxton turns manufacturing processes and advanced materials into miniature narrative mechanisms: chemically milled stainless steel business cards folding into 3D sculptures, electroluminescent clocks/tables, and intelligent objects. The business card example is especially useful: identity materializes through production technique.

Assa Ashuach connects digital modeling, stress analysis, selective laser sintering, and minimal material to surreal forms. His OMI.mgx lamp is not merely a curvy object; it depends on software and additive manufacturing. Function, structure, material economy, and aesthetic strangeness are calculated together.

Max Lamb goes the other direction: chiseling, burying, growing, smelting, carving polystyrene, sand-casting pewter. His work insists that material resistance and bodily labor can be as generative as CAD/CAM. It is a counterweight to frictionless digital fabrication.

Rule: material intelligence includes both computation and resistance. A good object should reveal what its material/process can uniquely do.

Sources: priority 399 Tord Boontje; 414 Viable; 429 Sam Buxton; 535 Assa Ashuach; 566 Max Lamb.

## 6. Domestic improvisation and nomadic objects: Peter Marigold, Committee, Julia Lohmann, Stuart Haygarth

Peter Marigold designs for nomadic living and temporary accommodation: shelving that expands into awkward gaps, crates that become storage after moving, poles and supports that avoid damaging rented walls. The design value is situational fit. Instead of assuming ideal architecture, it answers imperfect rooms and unstable lives.

Committee uses junk, market finds, skip materials, and local Deptford objects to make lighting and wallpaper. Their Kebab Lamps are not random bricolage; sequences are chosen to tell stories. The design intelligence is editing: salvage becomes narrative through arrangement.

Julia Lohmann uses animal by-products — tripe, hides, offal, leather waste — to expose contradictions in our relationship to animals as food/material. Her works oscillate between attraction and disgust. This is product design as social investigation: the object makes hidden material systems emotionally visible.

Stuart Haygarth creates chandeliers and lighting from everyday discarded objects: party poppers, flotsam, spectacles collected through Vision Aid Overseas. The key move is analogical transformation: glasses that once improved sight become a chandelier about vision/light. Salvage becomes meaning through collection, sorting, and recontextualization.

Rule: reuse design is weak if it only says “recycled.” It becomes strong when provenance, sequence, material memory, and new function reinforce each other.

Sources: priority 516 Peter Marigold; 560 Committee; 579 Julia Lohmann; 593 Stuart Haygarth.

## 7. Quiet rigorous product design and graphic publishing: Industrial Facility, FUEL, Michael Young

Industrial Facility (Sam Hecht and Kim Colin) is a model of quiet rigor. Their redesign of the Chantry knife sharpener shows the method: analyze how the object is used, then adjust casing, weight, context, and character so it belongs in the kitchen rather than looking like a machine fragment. Their work for Muji, Epson, Magis, Lexon, Whirlpool etc. fits an ethic of unobtrusive usefulness.

FUEL combines client work in fashion/music/art with self-initiated publishing. Its graphic work has a deliberately arresting, sometimes sinister quality. The important lesson is independence through publishing: a studio can build voice, archive, and cultural authority through self-initiated projects, not only client briefs.

Michael Young works with technically rigorous but humorous products. The Yogi furniture example is precise: low, cartoonish outdoor furniture makes adults feel incongruous and relax. Humor is not a surface gag; it changes posture and social behavior.

Rule: product character should be behaviorally grounded. Quietness, darkness, humor, or oddness should change how the object is used or read.

Sources: priority 467 Industrial Facility; 598 FUEL; 600 Michael Young.

## Consolidated additions to the design model

1. Interior architecture reminds us to design from occupation outward.
2. Infrastructure design coordinates engineering, politics, maintenance, and cultural imagination.
3. Game worlds are interactive UI systems, not just visual assets.
4. Social design must become affordance, network, lifecycle, or material structure.
5. Craft and computation are not opposites; both are ways to discover form.
6. Reuse needs provenance and narrative, not just recycled material.
7. Humor, grief, disgust, romance, and quietness are valid design materials when they alter use or perception.
8. Good design often starts with a non-ideal condition: rented rooms, disaster, waste, awkward gaps, outdated tools, unstable lives.
