## Main additions

### Social and service design

Hilary Cottam strengthens the social-design line: design as public service redesign, not only object making. This connects with Cameron Sinclair and Architecture for Humanity: design becomes valuable when it changes systems of care, housing, health, education, or social support.

Rule: service/social design should be judged by changed relationships and institutional behavior, not presentation polish.

### Product and furniture design

Jean Prouvé, Achille Castiglioni, BarberOsgerby, Jasper Morrison-adjacent topics, J. Mays, Flaminio Bertoni, Jaguar, and plywood/aluminium material entries strengthen the product-design backbone.

Prouvé links construction, prefabrication, furniture, and industrialized building. Castiglioni adds wit and readymade intelligence in product design. BarberOsgerby and related contemporary studios show industrial clarity and manufacturer collaboration. Automotive figures reinforce packaging, ergonomics, silhouette, and brand mythology as one system.

Rule: product design is strongest when material, manufacturing, use, and cultural reading become one decision field.

### Graphic and information design

Derek Birdsall, Kerr Noble, Edward McKnight Kauffer, Abram-Games-adjacent material, M/M, The Guardian, and Maureen Mooren/Daniel van der Velden add more graphic-design texture. The emphasis is editorial pacing, poster compression, institutional voice, fashion/graphic crossover, and print/digital publication systems.

Rule: graphic design maturity often appears in systems of rhythm: editions, covers, signage, campaigns, and recurring institutional touchpoints.

### Fashion, persona, and cultural worlds

Manolo Blahnik, Biba, Philip Treacy, John Galliano, Jamie Hewlett, M/M, Timorous Beasties, and the New York Times Paula Scher capture broaden the corpus beyond “design objects.” They show identity as persona, pattern, illustration, fashion performance, and fictional/cultural world-building.

Rule: in culture-facing design, a coherent world can be more powerful than a single logo or object.

### Architecture and spatial theory

Alison + Peter Smithson, Giles Gilbert Scott, Cedric Price, and Shin + Tomoko Azumi add multiple spatial attitudes: brutalist/social modernism, infrastructural civic objects, flexible/anti-monumental architecture, and product/space crossovers.

Cedric Price is especially important: architecture as enabling system rather than fixed monument. This fits the broader design model: the best systems create possibilities for users rather than freezing a single ideal behavior.

Rule: architecture can be judged by what it enables over time, not only what it looks like at opening.

### Material and experimental design

Plywood and aluminium entries, Hella Jongerius, Fernando + Humberto Campana, Ed Swan, Luis Eslava, Richard Sweeney, and Design Mart figures reinforce material experimentation. Materials are not neutral surfaces; their histories, technical limits, cultural status, and transformation processes carry meaning.

Rule: material choice should state what is being optimized: structure, comfort, cost, memory, repair, spectacle, sustainability, or emotional charge.
