Education By David Hannant Specialist reporter: health and education DHannant87 Share The ziggurats at the UEA remain out of use due to their crumbling concrete (Image: Bill Smith) They are instantly recognisable to UEA students past and present and are celebrated works of brutalist architecture. However, questions continue to shroud the future of the landmark ziggurats at the University of East Anglia. Since August 2023, the distinctive stepped structures, designed to house hundreds of student dwellings, have been empty after changes to government guidance on reinforced autoclaved aerated concrete (RAAC) designated them unsafe for use. Eighteen months on, their future remains unclear. University bosses have already spent around £2m in remedial works on the RAAC in parts of the Norfolk and Suffolk Terrace - as the ziggurats are officially called. But the UEA appears no closer to finding a permanent solution. A university spokesman said: "All students joining UEA in September will have university accommodation available to them, with options both on and off campus. "Options for the ziggurats are being explored and we are continuing to liaise with the local planning authority and Historic England to explore financially viable options that are safe for our students and meet the necessary requirements for Grade-II* listed buildings." The particular listing of the buildings, Grade II* means they are classified as having "importance of more than special interest" - a distinction held by less than 6pc of all listed buildings. It means that any alterations or improvements have to go through more stringent planning processes than non-listed buildings. This would in turn mean any changes have to be done in a very specific way to be sympathetic to its existing features. They were designed by architect Denys Lasdun and built in the 1960s and featured on the sleeve of the 2011 album Computer and Blues by The Streets.
