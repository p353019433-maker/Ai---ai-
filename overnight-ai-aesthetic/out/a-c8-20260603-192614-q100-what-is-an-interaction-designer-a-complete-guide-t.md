What does an interaction designer do? 6 interaction design dimensions Education requirements for an interaction designer Interaction design is a component of user experience (UX) design that creates interactions between users and products. As technology continues to advance and user experience becomes increasingly vital, interaction design has emerged as a key aspect of product development. If you're interested in interaction design, learning more about being a designer can help you determine if the role is right for you.In this article, we define an interaction designer, including what they do, review six interaction design dimensions and look over the education requirements, skills, work environment, job outlook and salary of the position. What is an interaction designer?An interaction designer ensures products function properly when users interact with them. They're responsible for understanding a user's needs and creating software, applications, websites and even physical products like smart devices.Interaction designers perform similar to work to user interface (UI) and UX designers. UX considers the interaction between a user and product as part of the user's entire experience, whereas interaction designers focus solely on how users interact with products. Some smaller organizations may assign interaction design responsibilities to their UX designers.Related: What Is UX design? And How To Become a UX Designer What does an interaction designer do?An interaction designer's responsibilities may vary by organization, especially depending on whether their organization also has UI and UX designers. These are some responsibilities an interaction designer may manage: Developing design strategy Implementing design features Creating and testing design prototypes Identifying a product's key interactions Improving digital functions Conducting user research Ensuring design meets accessibility standards Collaborating with UX and UI developersRelated: A Comprehensive Guide to Design Theory (With Tips) 6 interaction design dimensionsThese are some factors interaction designers consider when creating design strategy: 1. WordsWords include anything written in a design. Designs are best when the words are simple to understand and communicate the message clearly. For example, this may include clear and concise labels for buttons, informative error messages and context-sensitive help text. 2. Visual representationsThe dimension of visual representations includes all of a design's images and graphic elements. They often supplement what the design's words communicate. For example, this may include the consistent use of icons, color coding to indicate status or priority and visually distinct clickable elements.Read more: How To Make Website Graphics (With Benefits and Useful Tips) 3. Physical objects or spacePhysical objects or space refers to the physical items a user interacts with when using the product. This may involve imagining the situations a user may be in when using the product. This includes touchscreen design, interactive element placement and accommodations for different screen sizes. 4. TimeTime includes media that changes over time, including videos, sounds or animations. Interaction designers consider how the specific sound or motion may affect a user's interaction with the product. This includes button clicks and loading indicators.Read more: 18 Types of Videos You Can Use To Promote Your Business 5. BehaviorBehavior includes how users interact with products. This relates to how a user performs certain actions, operates an entire product or responds to using a product. It includes an intuitive navigational structure that accounts for swiping, tapping, pinching and scrolling. 6. HarmonyHarmony is the successful integration and coordination of all the other dimensions. It ensures words, visual representations, physical objects, time elements and behaviors collaborate to create a cohesive and enjoyable user experience. Interaction designers skillfully balance and intertwine these elements to deliver intuitive and engaging interactions for users. These educational resources often provide flexibility and accessibility, enabling learners to build a strong foundation in interaction design at their own pace.Related: FAQ: What Degree Does a UX Designer Need? Problem-solving skillsProblem-solving skills help interaction designers to evaluate problems and determine possible solutions. It's essential for them to have good problem-solving skills to identify ways to improve products. They also understand why problems occur and what solutions may best benefit users.
