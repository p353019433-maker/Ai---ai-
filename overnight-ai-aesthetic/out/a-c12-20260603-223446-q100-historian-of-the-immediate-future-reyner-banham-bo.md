Previous | Next The Independent Group, although, as Whiteley rightly states, an "all-inclusive" movement, initially had very little to do with architecture. In fact, its drive toward the dissolution of the distinctions between the media went against the very concept of architecture as a monumental, autonomous, constructed space. Banham's lifelong bent for things that functioned as a tease to architecture can be found in his celebration of portable gadgetry in "The Great Gizmo," in his focus on infrastructure over structure in The Architecture of the Well-Tempered Environment, and finally in the virtual disappearance of architecture into the sands of the Mojave Desert in his last book, Scenes of America Deserta. It can be found in nuce in his review of the exhibition This Is Tomorrow of 1956, where he wrote: The idea of synthesis is something the Pioneers of the Modern Movement inherited from the Academies and passed onto the Masters. When the theme was young, it was simple. Painting was going to lie down with Sculpture, and Architecture was to lie on top of them. But in passing from hand to hand, the theme has begun to split at the seams, some of the stuffing has come out, bats and bugs have got in, and it is by no means certain that architecture is still top dog, or that what sculptors and painters produce is necessarily painting or sculpture. (2) Advertisement Nonetheless, the Independent Group counted among its members Alison and Peter Smithson, and the movement claimed an architectural offspring, New Brutalism, the brainchild of Banham. By using the daunting term New Brutalism Banham meant to discredit the other new "isms"--New Humanism, New Empiricism, New Picturesque--that had happily sprung up in the aftermath of the war in England, embraced by an older generation in search of either redemption or a cure for the trauma of recent history. New Brutalism saw itself, through its antihumanism, as the first real break not only with the consoling ethics of the postwar years but also with the puritan ethics of the modern movement. Formally, it stood for a helter-skelter, impure approach against both the gentle vernacular espoused by the New Empiricists and the purism of the International Style. It is easy to see how Banham became an early enthusiast of Frank Gehry twenty years later. The singular ability of the English to reread the first historical avant-garde radically was what Philip Johnson had in mind when, in just two words, he commented on the Smithsons mastering of the "Mies vernacular" in their school at Hunstanton in Norfolk in 1954. (3) What sounded like an oxymoron, in view of Ludwig Mies van der Rohe's famously stripped-down minimalist vocabulary, was precisely the Smithsons' fear. The building became the linchpin of what Banham called New Brutalism. This was a building whose raw masonry, raw concrete, unpainted walls, and electric and heating appliances were all spectacularly exposed and for all that remained strikingly simple and enigmatic. Banham's intolerance for the slightest hint of romanticism is nowhere more evident than in his dismissive account of what would have appeared to any contemporary (and has been recently celebrated on the pages of the journals October and Grey Room) as one of the most daring instances of a derelict vernacular: the Smithsons' Patio and Pavilion at the exhibition This Is Tomorrow. This was an aluminum, plywood, and corrugated-plastic shed decorated with a grisly paper collage and urban junk. The structure can be best described as a wryly ironic postatomic shelter at the height of the Cold War and read as a parody of the urshelters that occasionally pop up in the history of high modernism, such as Le Corbusier's primitive hut in his book Une maison un palais (1928) and AlvarAalto's Forest Pavilion of 1938, as well as a satire on the eternally British "Home Sweet Home" style being revived in England by the proponents of the New Picturesque. Banham, however, read Patio and Pavilion as an instance of New Brutalism's submission to traditional values. He preferred the more futuristic pavilions in the show. The response to Banham's criticism was given by the Smithsons themselves with their aeronautic, capsulelike House of the Future at the Ideal Home exhibition in Londonjust a few months later.
